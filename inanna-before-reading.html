The Exaltation of Inanna

Before Reading

The historical and religious background needed to understand Enheduanna’s poem.

What Is This Poem?

The Exaltation of Inanna, also known by its Sumerian title Nin-me-šara, is a hymn traditionally attributed to Enheduanna. The poem begins by praising the terrifying power of the goddess Inanna, but it gradually becomes personal: Enheduanna describes her removal from religious office, her suffering in exile, and her appeal for divine assistance.

The work is therefore both a religious hymn and a personal response to a political crisis.

Who Was Enheduanna?

Enheduanna was a daughter of Sargon of Akkad, the ruler who established the Akkadian Empire. She was appointed high priestess of the moon god Nanna in the city of Ur.

This position was not merely religious. As high priestess, Enheduanna occupied an important political role connecting the Akkadian royal family with the powerful temples and cities of southern Mesopotamia.

She is often described as the earliest named author known from history because she identifies herself within works attributed to her.

Religion and Political Power

In ancient Mesopotamia, religion and politics were deeply connected. Kings claimed divine support, temples controlled significant wealth, and priests helped maintain relationships between cities, rulers, and gods.

Enheduanna’s appointment helped strengthen Akkadian authority in the Sumerian south. Her religious position therefore also supported her father’s political rule.

Who Is Inanna?

Inanna was a major Mesopotamian goddess associated with love, sexuality, warfare, political authority, and transformation. She is powerful because she can create prosperity, but she can also produce violence, destruction, and disorder.

In this poem, Enheduanna emphasizes Inanna’s fearsome side. The goddess overturns cities, defeats enemies, and exercises a power that even other divine figures struggle to oppose.

Why Does Enheduanna Turn to Inanna?

Enheduanna had been removed from her position during a rebellion connected with a ruler named Lugal-Ane. She describes being expelled from the temple and deprived of her authority.

She first appeals to Nanna, the god whose high priestess she is, but he does not immediately rescue her. She therefore turns to Inanna and asks the goddess to recognize her suffering, defeat her enemy, and restore her position.

What to Watch For

Changes in Voice

Notice how the poem moves from formal praise of a goddess to Enheduanna’s personal account of exile and suffering.

Descriptions of Power

Pay attention to the violent language used to describe Inanna’s authority over cities, armies, and other gods.

Religion and Government

Consider how Enheduanna uses divine authority to respond to a political attack against her.

Personal Authorship

Observe the moment when Enheduanna identifies herself and places her own experience inside a public religious poem.

Before You Begin

Do not read the poem only as praise of a goddess. It is also Enheduanna’s argument that the violence committed against her is an offense against the religious and political order.

Her restoration would therefore represent more than personal revenge: it would demonstrate that divine authority supports her position.

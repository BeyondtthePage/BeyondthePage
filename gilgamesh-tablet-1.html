Tablet I: The Coming of Enkidu

Gilgamesh, the suffering of Uruk, the creation of Enkidu, and the beginning of a transformative friendship.

← Back to The Epic of Gilgamesh

Tablet I at a Glance

Tablet I introduces Gilgamesh as both an extraordinary ruler and a serious problem for his own people. The citizens of Uruk appeal to the gods, who respond by creating Enkidu, a wild man strong enough to challenge him. After Enkidu is discovered living among animals, Shamhat is sent to draw him into human society. Meanwhile, Gilgamesh experiences dreams that predict the arrival of a powerful companion.

The tablet establishes several tensions that shape the entire epic: power and responsibility, civilization and wilderness, isolation and companionship, human action and divine intervention.

Detailed Sequence of Events

1. The opening praise of Gilgamesh

The epic begins by presenting Gilgamesh from the perspective of someone looking back on his completed life. He is introduced as a man who travelled widely, gained profound knowledge, endured great hardship, and restored knowledge from before the flood.

The narrator also draws attention to the walls of Uruk and invites the audience to examine the city’s construction. This creates a circular structure: Gilgamesh begins and eventually ends in Uruk. His lasting achievement is not personal immortality, but the city, story, and cultural memory associated with his name.

However, the opening description reflects the Gilgamesh who has already completed his journey. The story then moves backward to show that he did not begin as a wise ruler.

2. Gilgamesh as an oppressive king

Gilgamesh possesses unmatched physical strength and royal authority, but he does not exercise restraint. The people of Uruk complain that he dominates the young men and interferes with families and marriage.

The precise customs behind some of these complaints are debated, but the central point is clear: Gilgamesh’s power has become destructive. The people cannot oppose him directly, so they appeal to the gods.

This introduces one of the epic’s first major questions: what makes someone a legitimate ruler? Strength and divine ancestry give Gilgamesh authority, but they do not automatically make him just.

3. The gods decide to create Enkidu

The gods hear the complaints of Uruk. Aruru, who is associated with creation, forms Enkidu from clay or earth. He is designed as a counterpart to Gilgamesh: someone comparable in physical strength who can confront him and redirect his energy.

Enkidu is therefore not created simply as an enemy. His deeper role is to provide the equality and companionship that Gilgamesh lacks. The gods answer a political crisis by creating a relationship.

4. Enkidu in the wilderness

Enkidu initially lives among animals. He eats vegetation, drinks from watering places, and protects animals from hunters’ traps. He exists physically as a human but socially outside humanity.

His wilderness life is not presented as purely inferior. Enkidu is strong, free, and closely connected to the natural world. Yet he lacks human language, clothing, family, settled community, and awareness of social institutions.

This makes his later transformation complex. Entering civilization gives him knowledge and relationships, but it also permanently separates him from his earlier life.

5. The hunter discovers Enkidu

A hunter repeatedly encounters Enkidu near a watering place. Enkidu destroys or interferes with his traps, preventing him from hunting successfully.

The hunter is frightened because Enkidu appears extraordinarily powerful and unfamiliar. He reports the problem to his father, who advises him to seek help from Gilgamesh in Uruk.

This episode establishes the hunter as a representative of organized human activity. Enkidu’s protection of animals places him in direct conflict with hunting, tools, and human control over nature.

6. Shamhat is sent to meet Enkidu

Gilgamesh instructs the hunter to take Shamhat to the watering place. She is expected to use intimacy and human attraction to separate Enkidu from the animals.

Shamhat should not be reduced to a simple temptress. She acts as a mediator between the wilderness and the city. Through her, Enkidu begins to acquire speech, self-awareness, knowledge, and a place within human society.

7. Enkidu becomes separated from the animals

After his prolonged encounter with Shamhat, Enkidu attempts to return to the animals, but they flee from him. He can no longer run with them as he once did.

At first, this appears to be a physical weakening. Yet the text also presents it as the acquisition of a new kind of understanding. Enkidu has lost animal belonging but gained expanded consciousness.

His transformation is therefore not simply progress. It contains both gain and loss: knowledge replaces innocence, and human identity replaces complete unity with nature.

8. Shamhat tells Enkidu about Uruk and Gilgamesh

Shamhat praises Enkidu and invites him to enter Uruk. She describes the city, its people, its celebrations, and Gilgamesh’s extraordinary power.

Enkidu reacts strongly when he learns that Gilgamesh behaves without restraint. He wants to confront him and demonstrate that another man possesses comparable strength.

His journey toward Uruk now has both a social and moral purpose. He is entering civilization, but he is also entering it as a challenger to its ruler.

9. Gilgamesh’s dreams

In Uruk, Gilgamesh dreams of powerful objects falling from the sky. The people gather around them, and Gilgamesh feels an intense attraction toward them.

Ninsun interprets the dreams as signs that a strong companion is approaching. The imagery suggests that the relationship will not merely be practical. Gilgamesh will love and value this newcomer deeply.

The dreams create dramatic irony. The audience understands that Enkidu is moving toward Gilgamesh before the two men have met. What initially appears to be a coming rivalry is already being framed as a future friendship.

Difficult Ideas Explained

Why does the epic praise Gilgamesh before showing his cruelty?

The opening presents the completed Gilgamesh, not the immature ruler he was at the beginning of his life. The contrast allows the audience to see the epic as the story of how he became worthy of remembrance.

Why do the gods create a rival instead of punishing Gilgamesh?

Gilgamesh’s problem is not merely that he is powerful; it is that he has no equal who can restrain or redirect him. Enkidu becomes both a limit on his behavior and the companion through whom he begins to change.

Does Shamhat make Enkidu fully human?

Enkidu is already physically human, but he does not yet participate in human culture. His encounter with Shamhat begins the process by which he gains language, social awareness, clothing, prepared food, friendship, and knowledge of the city.

Why do the animals reject Enkidu?

His new human awareness breaks his former identity with them. The animals’ rejection represents an irreversible transition: Enkidu cannot gain civilization while remaining unchanged.

Why are Gilgamesh’s dreams important?

The dreams show that Enkidu’s arrival is part of a larger divine pattern. They also reveal that Gilgamesh unconsciously longs for an equal companion before he consciously understands that need.

Major Themes in Tablet I

Power without restraint

Gilgamesh’s strength makes him exceptional, but his lack of limits makes him harmful. The tablet separates physical greatness from moral or political maturity.

Civilization and wilderness

Uruk represents institutions, language, sexuality, labor, kingship, and collective human life. Enkidu’s wilderness represents freedom and closeness to nature. Neither world is presented as entirely simple or entirely good.

Friendship as transformation

Before Gilgamesh and Enkidu even meet, the tablet establishes that each is incomplete without the other. Their future friendship will redirect both lives.

Knowledge as gain and loss

Enkidu becomes more aware and more human, but this knowledge costs him his former innocence and belonging among the animals.

Divine intervention

The gods do not remove Gilgamesh or simply punish him. Instead, they alter human relationships by creating Enkidu, showing that divine intervention can work indirectly.

Why Tablet I Matters

Tablet I creates the conditions for the entire epic. It introduces Gilgamesh’s central flaw, Enkidu’s origin, the contrast between city and wilderness, and the relationship that will transform both men.

It also establishes the epic’s method: heroic action is never only adventure. Questions of power, identity, civilization, friendship, and mortality are already present before the major journeys begin.

Questions to Consider

  • Why is Gilgamesh admired and criticized at the same time?
  • What does Enkidu possess in the wilderness that civilized people do not?
  • What exactly does Enkidu gain after meeting Shamhat?
  • Why might companionship be a better solution to Gilgamesh’s behavior than direct punishment?
  • What do Gilgamesh’s dreams reveal about his emotional needs?
  • Does Tablet I portray civilization as progress, corruption, or a mixture of both?
